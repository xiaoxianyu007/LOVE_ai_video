Episode 1: The Rise of the Third Reich

In January 1933, a failed painter and charismatic orator became Chancellor of Germany. His name was Adolf Hitler, and within months he would dismantle the Weimar Republic and build a totalitarian state. The Reichstag fire of February 1933 gave him the pretext to suspend civil liberties. The Enabling Act passed in March granted him dictatorial powers, and by 1934, after the Night of the Long Knives, all opposition had been crushed. Germany was now a one-party state under the swastika.

Hitler's economic policies initially brought prosperity. The autobahn construction program employed hundreds of thousands of workers. Rearmament secretly began in violation of the Treaty of Versailles, with factories producing tanks, aircraft, and submarines. The Luftwaffe was unveiled in 1935, and compulsory military service was reintroduced. By 1936, the German army marched into the demilitarized Rhineland without facing any resistance from France or Britain. The West had chosen appeasement.

In March 1938, Germany annexed Austria in the Anschluss, absorbing the country without a single shot. The Sudetenland crisis followed in September, when Hitler demanded the German-speaking regions of Czechoslovakia. The Munich Agreement, signed by Britain, France, and Italy, handed over the Sudetenland in exchange for Hitler's promise of peace. Six months later, in March 1939, German troops occupied the rest of Czechoslovakia. The promise was broken, and Europe finally understood that Hitler could not be trusted.

The Nazi-Soviet Pact of August 1939 shocked the world. Two ideological enemies, Germany and the Soviet Union, signed a non-aggression treaty with a secret protocol dividing Eastern Europe between them. Poland was now surrounded. On September 1, 1939, German forces crossed the Polish border, using a staged attack on a German radio station as a pretext. Two days later, Britain and France declared war on Germany. The Second World War had begun.

Poland fell in just five weeks. The German Blitzkrieg, combining fast-moving tanks with close air support, overwhelmed Polish defenses. The Luftwaffe terrorized Warsaw with relentless bombing, reducing the city to rubble. Meanwhile, the Soviet Union invaded from the east, as agreed in the secret protocol. By October, Poland had ceased to exist as an independent nation, partitioned between two totalitarian powers. The world watched in horror as a new kind of warfare was unleashed upon Europe.

Episode 2: The Fall of France

After the Polish campaign, Europe entered a period of uneasy calm known as the Phoney War. Allied and German forces stared at each other across the French border for eight months. But in April 1940, the storm broke. Germany invaded Denmark and Norway in a lightning campaign, securing vital iron ore supplies and naval bases for the Atlantic war. Denmark fell in six hours. Norway held out for two months but was ultimately overwhelmed.

On May 10, 1940, the German offensive in the West began. Hitler launched Fall Gelb, the invasion of France and the Low Countries. Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg were attacked simultaneously. The Dutch army surrendered after five days of fighting, and Rotterdam was devastated by aerial bombardment. Belgium was quickly overrun, its fortifications bypassed by German paratroopers and glider-borne troops.

The French had placed their faith in the Maginot Line, a massive chain of fortifications along the German border. But the German plan was far more audacious. The main Panzer thrust came through the Ardennes Forest, a heavily wooded region that the French high command had considered impassable for tanks. General Heinz Guderian's Panzer corps smashed through the thin French defenses at Sedan and raced toward the English Channel, cutting the Allied armies in two.

The British Expeditionary Force, trapped with French units in a shrinking pocket, retreated to the port of Dunkirk. In one of the most dramatic rescues in military history, over three hundred thousand soldiers were evacuated across the Channel by a fleet of naval vessels and civilian boats. The Royal Air Force fought desperately to protect the evacuation, losing over a hundred aircraft. But the men were saved, though all their equipment was abandoned on the beaches.

On June 14, 1940, German troops marched into Paris unopposed. The French government fled to Bordeaux, and eight days later, an armistice was signed in the same railway carriage at Compiegne where Germany had surrendered in 1918. France was divided into an occupied zone in the north and a collaborationist Vichy regime in the south under Marshal Petain. In just six weeks, the German army had achieved what it had failed to do in four years during the First World War. The swastika flew over the Eiffel Tower, and Britain stood alone.

Episode 3: The Battle of Britain and Operation Barbarossa

With France defeated, Hitler expected Britain to seek peace. But Winston Churchill, who had become Prime Minister in May 1940, was determined to fight on. We shall never surrender, he told the British people and the world. The Battle of Britain began in July 1940, as the Luftwaffe launched a massive air campaign to destroy the Royal Air Force and pave the way for an invasion of England.

For three months, the skies over southern England witnessed relentless aerial combat. German bombers targeted airfields, radar stations, and aircraft factories. The British pilots, outnumbered and exhausted, fought with extraordinary courage. The Spitfire and Hurricane fighters became symbols of national resistance. The Dowding system, an integrated network of radar, observers, and centralized command, gave the RAF a critical advantage. By September, the Luftwaffe had suffered unsustainable losses and shifted its focus to bombing cities, in what became known as the Blitz.

The Blitz devastated London, Coventry, Liverpool, and other British cities. Tens of thousands of civilians were killed. But the Royal Air Force was not destroyed, and the invasion was postponed indefinitely. Hitler had suffered his first strategic defeat. His attention turned eastward, to the vast territories of the Soviet Union. Operation Barbarossa, the largest military invasion in history, was launched on June 22, 1941, with over three million German soldiers crossing the Soviet border along a front stretching from the Baltic to the Black Sea.

The initial advance was devastating. Soviet forces were caught completely by surprise, despite repeated warnings from intelligence sources that Stalin had ignored. Hundreds of thousands of Soviet soldiers were encircled and captured in massive pockets at Minsk, Smolensk, and Kiev. The German Panzers raced across the Russian steppe, covering hundreds of miles in weeks. By October, the Wehrmacht was at the gates of Moscow, and the Soviet government evacuated the capital. Victory seemed certain.

But then the Russian winter arrived. The German army, equipped for a summer campaign, was not prepared for temperatures that plunged to forty below zero. Engines froze, weapons jammed, and soldiers froze to death in their summer uniforms. The Soviet counteroffensive in December, led by fresh divisions from Siberia, pushed the Germans back from Moscow. For the first time in the war, the seemingly invincible Wehrmacht had been halted and forced to retreat. The myth of German invincibility was shattered.

Episode 4: Stalingrad and the Turning Point

In the summer of 1942, the German offensive resumed in the south, targeting the oil fields of the Caucasus and the industrial city of Stalingrad on the Volga River. Hitler divided his forces, a fatal strategic error. The Sixth Army under General Friedrich Paulus was ordered to capture Stalingrad, a city that bore Stalin's name and thus carried immense symbolic importance for both sides. The battle that followed would become the bloodiest in human history.

The fighting in Stalingrad was unlike anything the world had seen. German soldiers fought block by block, room by room, through the ruins of a city reduced to rubble by Luftwaffe bombing. Soviet defenders held on with desperate tenacity, often fighting for weeks in the same destroyed factory. The average life expectancy of a Soviet soldier in the city was less than twenty-four hours. Civilians who could not escape were trapped in the hell of urban warfare, hiding in cellars as the battle raged above them.

In November 1942, the Soviets launched Operation Uranus, a massive counteroffensive that encircled the German Sixth Army in Stalingrad. The encirclement trapped over two hundred thousand German soldiers in a pocket roughly the size of Manhattan. Hitler forbade any breakout attempt, insisting that the Luftwaffe could supply the trapped army by air. But the airlift was a catastrophic failure. The German soldiers inside the pocket, known as the Kessel, suffered from starvation, frostbite, and disease in temperatures that reached thirty below zero.

By January 1943, the situation inside the Kessel was desperate. Soldiers were eating horses, dogs, and rats. Ammunition was running out. The Soviet army tightened its grip, launching a final assault to crush the pocket. On February 2, 1943, Paulus surrendered, becoming the first German Field Marshal in history to be captured alive. Of the three hundred thousand men who had entered Stalingrad, only ninety thousand remained to surrender. Barely five thousand would survive Soviet captivity and return to Germany after the war.

The defeat at Stalingrad was a catastrophe for Germany. It marked the end of the German advance in the East and the beginning of the long retreat that would end in Berlin. The news sent shockwaves through the Reich, and Goebbels declared total war in his infamous Sportpalast speech. Meanwhile, in North Africa, the British Eighth Army defeated Rommel at El Alamein, and the Allies landed in Morocco and Algeria. The tide of the war had truly turned, and the Axis powers were now on the defensive everywhere.

Episode 5: D-Day and the Fall of the Third Reich

By 1944, the Allies were ready to open the long-awaited second front in Western Europe. Operation Overlord, the largest amphibious invasion in history, was planned for the coast of Normandy. General Dwight Eisenhower was appointed Supreme Commander of the Allied forces. The invasion involved over one hundred and fifty thousand troops, thousands of ships, and hundreds of aircraft. The fate of Europe hung on the success of this single, massive operation.

On the morning of June 6, 1944, Allied paratroopers landed behind enemy lines to secure bridges and disrupt German communications. At dawn, the first waves of infantry hit the beaches of Normandy under withering German fire. The American beaches of Omaha and Utah saw ferocious combat, with Omaha becoming a killing zone where hundreds of soldiers were cut down before reaching the shore. The British and Canadian forces at Gold, Juno, and Sword beaches faced heavy resistance but pushed inland. By the end of the day, the Allies had established a foothold on the continent.

The Normandy campaign was a brutal war of attrition. For weeks, the Allies fought through the bocage country, a maze of ancient hedgerows that made every field a potential death trap. German defenders, many of them hardened veterans of the Eastern Front, fought with desperate skill. But the Allied numerical superiority in men, tanks, and aircraft was overwhelming. The breakout finally came in late July with Operation Cobra, which shattered the German lines and allowed Patton's Third Army to race across France.

Paris was liberated on August 25, 1944, to scenes of wild celebration. The German army retreated across France, and by September, the Allies had reached the German border. But the advance stalled as supplies ran short. In December, Hitler launched a desperate counteroffensive through the Ardennes, the same forest where Germany had achieved its great victory in 1940. The Battle of the Bulge was the largest battle fought by the US Army in the war, costing over eighty thousand American casualties. But the German offensive was eventually contained and pushed back.

In the spring of 1945, the Allies crossed the Rhine and drove into the heart of Germany. In the east, the Soviet Red Army advanced relentlessly toward Berlin, exacting a terrible revenge for the suffering inflicted on their homeland. On April 30, 1945, with Soviet troops just blocks from his bunker, Adolf Hitler committed suicide. Berlin fell on May 2, and Germany surrendered unconditionally on May 7. The war in Europe was over after six years of unimaginable destruction, leaving tens of millions dead and a continent in ruins.
