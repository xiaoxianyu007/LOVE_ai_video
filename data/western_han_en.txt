Episode 1: Lee-oh Bahng's Coronation at Ding-taow

In the winter of 202 BC, the blood of Shyahng Yoo's army still stained the fields of Gai-shee-ah.
The Choo-Han Contention, a brutal four-year civil war, had finally reached its bloody end.
Lee-oh Bahng, a commoner who rose from a village headman, now stood as the master of all under Heaven.
Seven feudal kings gathered at Ding-taow, urging him to take the imperial throne.
They declared that his achievements surpassed all rulers since the beginning of recorded history.
Lee-oh Bahng modestly declined three times, as ritual demanded, before accepting the Mandate of Heaven.
On the banks of the Fahn River, he ascended the throne and proclaimed the Han Dynasty.
The ceremony was simple and solemn, befitting a man who had spent years fighting on horseback.
He named his dynasty Han after the remote Hahn-joong region where his journey to power began.
The new emperor was fifty-four years old, his body scarred by countless battles.
He chose Lwo-yahng as his provisional capital, but this decision would soon be challenged.
A dynasty was born, destined to rule China for over four centuries.

Episode 2: Building the Capital at Chahng-ahn

The Han Dynasty had a throne, but it did not yet have a true capital.
A low-ranking cavalry officer named Lo Jing approached the emperor with a bold proposal.
He argued that the Gwahn-joong region, with its natural barriers and fertile soil, was the true heartland of power.
Lee-oh Bahng consulted his advisors, and the great strategist Jahng Lee-ahng strongly endorsed Lo Jing's vision.
The emperor ordered a grand new capital built on the ruins of the old Chin capital Shyen-yahng.
Workers laid foundations for Chahng-luh Palace, a magnificent complex of halls and pavilions.
The palace was built on the ruins of the Chin Dynasty's Shing-luh Palace, a symbolic triumph.
Shyaow Huh, the chancellor, personally oversaw the construction of the even grander Way-yahng Palace.
When Lee-oh Bahng saw the scale of Way-yahng Palace, he rebuked Shyaow Huh for excessive extravagance.
Shyaow Huh replied that a dynasty without a majestic palace commands no awe from its subjects.
The capital was named Chahng-ahn, meaning Eternal Peace, a hope for the centuries to come.
Chahng-ahn would grow into the greatest city on earth, a glittering center of civilization.
Its walls and gates welcomed traders, scholars, and envoys from across the known world.

Episode 3: Eliminating the Kings: Death of Hahn Sheen

Hahn Sheen was the greatest military genius of his age, the architect of Lee-oh Bahng's victory over Shyahng Yoo.
He commanded armies that conquered the kingdoms of Way, Dai, Jow, and Yan with breathtaking speed.
At the Battle of Jing-shing, he defeated a force ten times his size with a masterful riverbank trap.
He was first made King of Chee, then transferred to become King of Choo, a demotion that sowed deep resentment.
In 201 BC, Lee-oh Bahng accused him of plotting rebellion and reduced him to the lowly title of Marquis of Hwai-yin.
The great general, now a humiliated nobleman, spent his days brooding in the capital under constant surveillance.
In 196 BC, while Lee-oh Bahng was away suppressing a rebellion, Empress Lyoo Juh made her move.
She conspired with Chancellor Shyaow Huh, the very man who had once recommended Hahn Sheen to Lee-oh Bahng.
Shyaow Huh lured Hahn Sheen to Chahng-luh Palace with a false message that the emperor had returned victorious.
Hahn Sheen entered the palace, trusting the man who had discovered him, and walked into the Bell Chamber.
There, Empress Lyoo's guards seized him, and he was executed on the spot without a trial.
His last words were a bitter lament: I regret not following Kwai Toong's advice.
The empress then ordered the extermination of Hahn Sheen's entire clan, three generations erased in a single day.
A military genius who had never lost a battle fell not to an enemy army, but to palace intrigue.
The death of Hahn Sheen sent a chilling message to every feudal king in the empire.

Episode 4: Pung Yweh and Ying Boo

The stench of blood filled the imperial kitchen, and the cooks were preparing something unspeakable.
Pung Yweh, the King of Liang and a former ally, had been reduced to a mound of minced flesh.
His remains were seasoned, cooked, and distributed in sealed jars to every feudal king across the empire.
They opened the jars with trembling hands, and the message was unmistakable: obey or become the next dish.
This was the spring of 196 BC, and the founding emperor was systematically destroying his former comrades.
Pung Yweh had been accused of conspiracy, a charge he denied until his final breath.
Lee-oh Bahng stripped him of his titles and exiled him to the remote Shu region.
On the road to exile, Pung Yweh encountered Empress Lyoo, who offered to intercede on his behalf.
She brought him back to the capital, then told her husband that Pung Yweh was too dangerous to leave alive.
Lee-oh Bahng had Pung Yweh executed, his body mutilated, and his flesh distributed as a warning.
When Ying Boo, the King of Huainan, received his jar of human meat, he knew his turn had come.
He mobilized his battle-hardened army, veterans of countless campaigns, and declared open rebellion.
The aging Lee-oh Bahng, now sixty-one years old, personally led the imperial army to crush the revolt.
During the battle, a stray arrow struck the emperor, a wound that would slowly kill him.
Ying Boo's forces were annihilated, and the last of the great independent kings was hunted down and killed.
Within five years, Lee-oh Bahng had eliminated every major feudal king not of the Liu clan.

Episode 5: The Siege at Bai-dung

In the autumn of 200 BC, King Sheen of Hahn surrendered his territory to the Shyong-noo and defected to the steppe.
Lee-oh Bahng was furious, and he personally led three hundred twenty thousand soldiers north to punish the traitor.
The Han army marched deep into the frozen grasslands, chasing the Shyong-noo cavalry across the endless plains.
Mo-doo Chahn-yoo, the brilliant Shyong-noo leader, had unified the northern nomads into a terrifying military machine.
He commanded four hundred thousand elite horsemen, each trained from childhood to ride and shoot with deadly precision.
Lee-oh Bahng, impatient and overconfident, raced ahead of his main army with only a vanguard force.
At Mount Bai-dung, near the modern city of Dah-toong, the trap snapped shut around the emperor.
The Shyong-noo cavalry surrounded the mountain, cutting off all escape and supply routes.
The emperor was trapped for seven days and seven nights, his soldiers freezing and starving on the barren slopes.
Chun Ping, the emperor's chief strategist, devised a desperate plan to bribe Mo-doo's favorite wife.
He sent lavish gifts and a portrait of a beautiful Han princess, warning that Shyong-noo women would lose favor.
The Shyong-noo queen, fearing her replacement, persuaded Mo-doo to open a gap in the encirclement.
On the seventh day, a thick fog descended, and Lee-oh Bahng's forces slipped through the opening.
The emperor had survived, but the myth of Han invincibility was shattered forever.
The Siege at Bai-dung forced the Han Dynasty to adopt a humiliating policy of appeasement for decades.

Episode 6: The White Horse Covenant

The year was 195 BC, and Lee-oh Bahng was dying from the arrow wound he received fighting Ying Boo.
His body was failing, but his mind was consumed by a single obsession: the survival of the Liu clan.
He had spent years eliminating the great feudal kings, but he knew the threat was not yet extinguished.
The emperor summoned his most trusted generals and ministers to the ancestral temple.
A white horse was led to the altar, its coat gleaming like fresh snow under the ceremonial torches.
Lee-oh Bahng drew his sword and slit the horse's throat, the blood pouring into a bronze vessel.
He raised the vessel and swore an oath that would echo through the ages.
Henceforth, if any man not of the Liu clan is made a king, the entire empire shall rise and strike him down.
Every minister and general present drank the blood and swore to honor the covenant.
The White Horse Covenant was the founding emperor's final act of statecraft.
Lee-oh Bahng died soon after, leaving a throne secured by blood, iron, and sacred oath.
His son Lee-oh Ying, the Crown Prince, ascended the throne and became Emperor Hui of Han.
But the real power behind the throne was already moving into position.

Episode 7: Empress Lyoo's Regency

A woman's scream echoed through the halls of the imperial palace, a sound that froze the blood of all who heard it.
Empress Lyoo Juh stood over a mutilated figure that had once been the most beautiful woman in the empire.
Concubine Chee had no hands, no feet, no eyes, and no tongue, yet she was somehow still alive, writhing in a jar.
Empress Lyoo smiled and called her son Emperor Hui to come and admire her creation, the human pig.
The timid young emperor vomited on the spot and never recovered from the horror of what he witnessed.
This was the year 195 BC, and the reign of terror had begun the moment Lee-oh Bahng closed his eyes.
Empress Lyoo Juh was the first woman in Chinese history to rule the empire as regent.
She had been Lee-oh Bahng's wife since his days as a village headman, enduring hardship and humiliation with him.
Now, with her husband dead and her son too weak to rule, Lyoo Juh seized absolute power.
She systematically poisoned, strangled, or drove to suicide every son of Lee-oh Bahng born to other consorts.
Three young princes of the Liu clan, sons of the founding emperor, were murdered by her orders.
The empress promoted her own Lyoo clan relatives to the highest offices, making them kings and generals.
She ruled for fifteen years, maintaining stability at the price of rivers of Liu blood.
The Han Dynasty survived, but the question was: whose dynasty was it now?

Episode 8: Eliminating the Lyoo Clan

In the summer of 180 BC, Empress Lyoo Juh died, and the empire held its breath.
The Lyoo clan, now entrenched in every corner of the government and military, prepared to seize full control.
Joe Bwo, the Grand Commandant of the Han army, saw the impending coup and swore to stop it.
He was a veteran of the founding wars, a man who had bled alongside Lee-oh Bahng at Gai-shee-ah.
Together with Chancellor Chun Ping, he hatched a daring plan to eliminate the Lyoo clan in one stroke.
They tricked Lyoo Chahn, the most powerful Lyoo general, into handing over his military seal.
Joe Bwo then entered the northern army camp and made a simple declaration to the soldiers.
Those who support the Liu clan, bare your left shoulder; those who support the Lyoo clan, bare your right.
Every soldier in the camp bared his left shoulder, and the army roared its allegiance to the Liu clan.
The Lyoo clan was rounded up and executed, every man, woman, and elder associated with the usurpers.
The purge was swift, brutal, and absolute, ensuring the Lyoo name would never threaten the throne again.
The ministers then faced a difficult question: who should be the next emperor?
They chose Lee-oh Hung, the King of Dai, a quiet prince whose mother was a minor consort with no powerful relatives.
A messenger was dispatched to the remote kingdom of Dai, carrying an invitation that would change history.

Episode 9: Emperor Wen's Accession

A group of imperial envoys arrived at the remote court of Dai with an extraordinary offer: the throne of the empire.
Lee-oh Hung, the King of Dai, was a cautious man who had survived the Lyoo terror by keeping a low profile.
He was the fourth son of Lee-oh Bahng, born to a concubine so minor that her name was barely recorded.
When the envoys knelt before him, Lee-oh Hung did not celebrate; he was deeply suspicious.
He consulted his advisors, who were divided: some saw a golden opportunity, others saw a deadly trap.
Lee-oh Hung sent his uncle to the capital first to verify that the offer was genuine.
Only after receiving confirmation did he set out for Chahng-ahn, his heart heavy with uncertainty.
When he arrived at the Way River Bridge, the ministers of the court knelt to welcome him as emperor.
Lee-oh Hung stopped at the bridge and refused to cross, testing whether their deference was sincere.
Joe Bwo stepped forward and presented the imperial seal, but Lee-oh Hung still declined three times.
Only after the ritual of refusal was complete did he accept the throne as Emperor Wen of Han.
He was twenty-three years old, and a new chapter in Chinese history was about to begin.
His first act was to secure the palace, replacing the Lyoo guards with his own trusted men.
The Wun-Jing era, one of the most celebrated golden ages in Chinese history, had quietly begun.

Episode 10: Light Taxes, Light Corvée

A young girl named Chun-yoo Tiying knelt before the emperor, clutching a letter soaked with her tears.
Her father, a respected physician, had been wrongly convicted and was sentenced to mutilating punishment.
She had traveled hundreds of miles on foot, this girl of barely fifteen, to plead for her father's life.
Her letter moved the entire court, and Emperor Wen read it with visible emotion.
This was the Han Dynasty under Emperor Wen, a ruler who governed with compassion rather than fear.
He abolished the law of collective punishment, ending the practice of executing entire families for one person's crime.
He reduced the land tax to one-fifteenth of the harvest, and at one point abolished it entirely for twelve years.
He abolished mutilating punishments, including facial branding, cutting off the nose, and amputating the foot.
The emperor himself wore rough silk robes and straw sandals, refusing to spend on personal luxuries.
His palace had no new extensions, and his tomb was furnished only with pottery, not gold or jade.
He ordered that no corvée labor be requisitioned during the farming seasons, respecting the rhythm of the land.
The granaries overflowed, the people multiplied, and the empire began to heal from decades of war.
Emperor Wen's reign was not dramatic, it was not filled with great battles or grand conquests.
But in his quiet, steady governance, he laid the foundation for a dynasty that would endure for four hundred years.
The Wun-Jing era was a time of peace, prosperity, and the slow, patient rebuilding of a civilization.

Episode 11: Tiying Saves Her Father and Abolition of Mutilating Punishment

In 167 BC, Chun-yoo Yee, the granary official of Chee, was denounced and sent to Chahng-ahn for trial.
He was a skilled physician who had angered powerful nobles by refusing to treat them.
The court sentenced him to mutilating punishment: branding, nose-cutting, or foot amputation.
Chun-yoo Yee had five daughters, and the youngest was named Tiying, just fifteen years old.
Watching her father in chains, Tiying made a courageous decision.
She followed the prison cart on foot for a thousand miles, all the way from Chee to Chahng-ahn.
Tiying submitted a petition to Emperor Wen: I offer myself as a slave to redeem my father's punishment.
She wrote that her father was an honest official who deserved a chance to reform himself.
Emperor Wen read her tear-stained letter and was deeply moved by its sincerity.
He issued an edict: Punishments that sever limbs and scar flesh leave no hope, and are unworthy of a virtuous ruler.
He ordered the abolition of mutilating punishments and replaced them with alternative penalties.
Branding was replaced by shaved-head hard labor, nose-cutting by three hundred strokes, and foot amputation by five hundred.
The courage of a fifteen-year-old girl changed the brutal tradition of punishment in China.
The story of Tiying saving her father became an enduring symbol of filial devotion.

Episode 12: Rebellion of the Seven States – Part 1

Chow Tswo stood before the court and presented a memorial that would shake the empire.
He urged the reduction of vassal fiefs, arguing that powerful feudal kings threatened the central government.
Chow Tswo told Emperor Jing: Cut their lands and they rebel; do nothing and they still rebel, but cutting them brings a quicker end.
King Lee-oh Bee of Wu ruled the largest fief, rich with copper mines and sea salt, wealthier than the imperial treasury.
In 154 BC, King Lee-oh Bee of Wu allied with Choo, Jow, and four other kingdoms, raising an army to clear the court of evil ministers.
The rebel coalition, blaming Chow Tswo for all ills, marched hundreds of thousands of troops toward Chahng-ahn.
Chow Tswo's father traveled from Ying-chwahn, knelt before his son, and pleaded with tears in his eyes.
He warned that offending the feudal lords would bring ruin: the Liu clan will be safe, but the Chow clan will perish.
Chow Tswo replied: Without this, the emperor has no authority and the ancestral temple is not secure.
The old father returned home and drank poison, unwilling to witness the destruction of his family.
Emperor Jing was gripped by panic and urgently summoned Chow Tswo to discuss strategy.
Chow Tswo shockingly suggested the emperor himself lead the campaign while he remained in the capital.
The court seethed with resentment, and a conspiracy against Chow Tswo was already taking shape.

Episode 13: Rebellion of the Seven States – Part 2

Do Ying recommended Ywen Ahng to Emperor Jing, a man who had served as chancellor of Wu and knew the rebels well.
Ywen Ahng met the emperor alone, dismissed all attendants, and made a shocking proposal.
He argued that since the rebels claimed to target only Chow Tswo, killing him would strip them of their cause.
Emperor Jing sat in silence for a long moment, then made his decision.
Days later, Chow Tswo was seized in his court robes and dragged to the execution ground at the eastern market.
He believed he was being summoned to discuss state affairs, unaware that he was walking to his death.
The executioner struck, and Chow Tswo was cut in half at the waist, his blood soaking the execution ground.
The rebellion did not stop after his death, and Emperor Jing was consumed by regret.
Joe Yah-foo, the Grand Commandant, was given command of the imperial army to face the rebel forces.
He used the kingdom of Liang as bait, holding his position at Chahng-yee while cutting the enemy supply lines.
He sent light cavalry deep behind enemy lines to burn the rebels' grain and fodder.
As the rebels starved and scattered, Joe Yah-foo pressed the attack and crushed the rebellion in three months.
All seven kingdoms were dissolved, and their kings were executed or driven to suicide.
Central authority was massively strengthened, and vassal kings lost all power to govern or command troops.

Episode 14: Emperor Jing's Legacy

The smoke of the Rebellion of the Seven States had cleared, and Emperor Jing stood alone amid the ruins.
He had inherited his father's benevolent policies, but faced bloodshed his father had never known.
Emperor Jing continued to reduce vassal power, stripping feudal kings of all administrative authority.
From then on, officials in vassal states were appointed by the central government, and kings merely collected tax revenues.
Emperor Jing was a pivotal transitional figure, the one who truly consolidated the Wun-Jing legacy.
He maintained light taxes and corvée, and the state treasuries and granaries overflowed with abundance.
Irrigation projects were built across the empire, and agricultural production steadily expanded.
In the Chahng-ahn treasury, copper coins were piled so high that the strings holding them rotted and snapped.
He was sharp-tongued yet open to remonstrance, once driving the crown prince to suicide over a minor dispute.
This emperor was far from perfect, but in sixteen years of rule he did one thing right: he preserved the dynasty.
In 141 BC, Emperor Jing fell gravely ill, and he knew his time was running out.
He summoned the crown prince Lee-oh Chuh, a boy of sixteen, who knelt at his father's bedside.
Emperor Jing took his son's hand and entrusted him with a unified and powerful empire.
Emperor Jing passed away, and Crown Prince Lee-oh Chuh ascended the throne as Emperor Wu of Han.

Episode 15: Young Emperor Wu Ascends

In the first year of the Jyen-ywen era, a storm was gathering over the young emperor's face in the Chahng-ahn palace.
Lee-oh Chuh had just ascended the throne at sixteen, and he was impatient to launch his reform program.
He appointed the Confucian scholars Jow Wahn as Imperial Secretary and Wahng Dzahng as Director of the Palace Guard.
The two Confucian officials proposed building a Hall of Light to govern the empire with ancient ritual.
But deep in the palace, an old woman with white hair watched all of this with cold disapproval.
Grand Empress Dowager Dou was the widow of Emperor Wen, the mother of Emperor Jing, and the grandmother of Lee-oh Chuh.
She was a devout follower of Hwahng-Laow Daoism and believed Confucian rituals were wasteful and disruptive.
An edict issued from her palace, and both Jow Wahn and Wahng Dzahng were arrested and thrown into prison.
The two Confucian ministers committed suicide in their cells, and the Jyen-ywen reforms were completely reversed.
The young emperor tasted the bitterness of power for the first time, and he could do nothing but wait.
He built the Shahng-lin Park west of Chahng-ahn, where he rode horses and practiced archery, secretly building his strength.
Way Ching, a young man born of a slave, first came to Lee-oh Chuh's attention in the grounds of Shahng-lin.
In the sixth year of Jyen-ywen, 135 BC, Grand Empress Dowager Dou finally passed away.
On the first day of his personal rule, Lee-oh Chuh stood on the high terrace of Way-yahng Palace and gazed over Chahng-ahn.
The most magnificent chapter in Chinese history was about to begin.

Episode 16: Confucianism as State Ideology

In 135 BC, Grand Empress Dowager Dou died, and Emperor Wu finally took personal control of the empire.
He adopted Doong Joong-shoo's Three Strategies of Heaven and Man, establishing Confucianism as the sole state ideology.
Doong Joong-shoo elaborated the theory of the divine right of kings in his famous memorial.
He proposed the doctrine of Heaven-human interaction, merging Confucianism with yin-yang and the five elements.
This system created a complete ideological framework for the imperial state.
An imperial academy was established in Chahng-ahn, selecting officials based on Confucian classics.
Chancellor Way Wahn petitioned to ban Legalist and Political Strategist teachings, and the emperor approved.
Goong-swoon Hawng, a commoner, rose to become chancellor and was ennobled solely for his mastery of the Spring and Autumn Annals.
The court established Erudites for the Five Classics, teaching the Odes, Documents, Rites, Changes, and Annals.
Henceforth, scholars across the empire pursued the Confucian classics as their path to official rank.
Confucianism became the orthodox ideology of China for the next two thousand years.
It laid the intellectual foundation for the civil service system, shaping governance for centuries to come.

Episode 17: The Mah-yee Scheme

Outside the city of Mah-yee, in the pitch-black night, three hundred thousand Han soldiers lay in silent ambush.
The Shyong-noo Chahn-yoo's one hundred thousand cavalry had entered the trap, but a single detail would change history.
Wahng Hway, the Grand Herald, proposed luring the Shyong-noo deep into Han territory to annihilate their main army.
Nyeh Yee, a Mah-yee merchant, was sent to feign surrender and lure the Shyong-noo Chahn-yoo southward.
The Chahn-yoo led his elite cavalry of one hundred thousand across the Great Wall in full force.
But the Shyong-noo scouts noticed something strange: livestock grazed unattended, but there was not a single person in sight.
The Chahn-yoo's suspicion grew, and he ordered an attack on a border watchtower to investigate.
They captured a low-ranking officer from Yen-mun, who revealed the entire ambush plan under brutal torture.
The Chahn-yoo was horrified and immediately ordered a full retreat back to the steppe.
The three hundred thousand Han soldiers waited in vain and returned home with nothing to show.
Wahng Hway took his own life, accepting full responsibility for the operation's catastrophic failure.
The Mah-yee Scheme ended in failure, but it marked the formal rupture between Han and the Shyong-noo.
The policy of appeasement was abandoned forever, and total war between the two powers had begun.

Episode 18: Rise of Way Ching

In 129 BC, Emperor Wu launched a four-pronged offensive against the Shyong-noo.
Way Ching marched from Shahng-goo and struck directly at the Shyong-noo sacred city of Loong-chung, taking seven hundred heads.
This was the first military victory the Han Dynasty had ever achieved against the Shyong-noo.
Way Ching was born into slavery, the son of a bondwoman in the household of the Princess of Ping-yahng.
He grew up herding sheep, enduring constant humiliation and contempt from everyone around him.
His elder sister Way Dzuh-foo became a favored consort of Emperor Wu, and Way Ching's life was transformed.
The emperor summoned him to the palace and discovered a man of calm nerve and natural command.
In 127 BC, Way Ching led a major campaign to recapture the Ordos Loop, the land south of the Yellow River bend.
The Han established Shwo-fahng and Woo-ywen commanderies, settling one hundred thousand colonists to farm and defend the frontier.
Way Ching was enfeoffed as the Marquis of Chahng-ping, rising from a slave to the empire's supreme general.
His legend embodied the spirit of the Wu era, where talent mattered more than birth.

Episode 19: Huh-shee Corridor Campaign

Hwo Choo-bing led ten thousand cavalry out of Loong-shee in a lightning campaign deep into Shyong-noo territory.
In six days he covered over a thousand miles, crossing the Yen-juh Mountains as if they were undefended.
He slew the kings of Juh-lahn and Luhou and captured the son of the Hunye King.
That same summer, Hwo Choo-bing launched a second western campaign, crossing the Chee-lyen Mountains two thousand miles deep.
He took over thirty thousand heads, and the Shyong-noo presence in the Huh-shee Corridor was shattered.
The Shyong-noo sang a lament: We lost our Chee-lyen Mountains, and our herds can no longer thrive.
We lost our Yen-juh Mountains, and our women have lost their beauty, the song spread across the steppe.
The Hunye King killed the Shyoo-too King and surrendered with forty thousand of his people to the Han.
The Han established four commanderies in the Huh-shee Corridor: Woo-way, Jahng-yeh, Jyoh-chwen, and Doon-hwahng.
The Huh-shee Corridor was incorporated into Chinese territory, and the gateway to the Western Regions was opened.

Episode 20: Hwo Choo-bing's Blitzkrieg

In 119 BC, for the decisive Mwo-bay campaign, Hwo Choo-bing led fifty thousand cavalry from Dai Commandery.
He rode two thousand miles deep into the northern desert to engage the Shyong-noo's Wise King of the Left in battle.
After crushing the enemy, Hwo Choo-bing ascended Mount Lahng-joo-shoo to perform the Feng sacrifice to Heaven.
He performed the Shan sacrifice at Mount Goo-yen, then reached Lake Baikal, known to the ancients as the Vast Sea.
The Feng sacrifice at Lahng-joo-shoo became the supreme symbol of military glory in Chinese history.
Hwo Choo-bing was a man of few words but extraordinary courage, decisive and fearless in battle.
When Emperor Wu built him a grand mansion, he declined, saying: The Shyong-noo are not yet destroyed, why should I have a home?
In 117 BC, Hwo Choo-bing died suddenly of illness at the age of only twenty-three.
Emperor Wu was devastated with grief and buried him beside his own future tomb at the Maow-ling mausoleum.
His tomb was shaped like the Chee-lyen Mountains, a memorial to his campaigns in the Huh-shee Corridor.

Episode 21: Battle of Mwo-bay

In 119 BC, Way Ching and Hwo Choo-bing each led fifty thousand cavalry deep into the northern desert to seek a decisive battle.
Way Ching's army marched over a thousand miles beyond the frontier and finally encountered the main force of the Shyong-noo Chahn-yoo.
He ordered his war chariots to form a circular fortification, then sent five thousand horsemen to test the enemy's strength.
At dusk, a violent sandstorm swept across the battlefield, and the two armies could barely see each other.
Way Ching seized the moment, dispatching cavalry from both flanks to envelop the Shyong-noo formation.
The Chahn-yoo, seeing the overwhelming Han forces, fled with a few hundred elite riders under cover of the storm.
The Han army pursued the enemy to Jow Sheen City at Mount Tyen-yen, burning the Shyong-noo's stored grain before returning.
After this battle, the Shyong-noo's main army was shattered, and no Shyong-noo royal court was ever seen south of the desert again.
The veteran general Lee Gwahng repeatedly begged to lead the vanguard, but Emperor Wu considered him too old and unlucky.
The emperor secretly instructed Way Ching not to give Lee Gwahng any critical front-line role.
Lee Gwahng lost his way in the desert and failed to join the main battle against the Chahn-yoo.
After the battle, Way Ching sent investigators to determine the cause, and Lee Gwahng, overwhelmed by shame, drew his sword and took his own life.
The Flying General Lee Gwahng, a legendary warrior of his age, met a tragic end in the vast desert.
The Battle of Mwo-bay was a glorious victory, but it had drained the Han Dynasty's national strength.
Of the one hundred forty thousand warhorses that set out on the campaign, fewer than thirty thousand returned.
The Han Dynasty was now too exhausted to launch another large-scale expedition for years to come.

Episode 22: Jahng Chyen's Journey to the West

Jahng Chyen stood before the royal court of the Greater Yweh-juh, gazing silently at the endless desert to the west.
He had waited for this moment for ten long years, imprisoned in the hands of the Shyong-noo.
In 138 BC, Emperor Wu of Han recruited envoys to forge an alliance with the Greater Yweh-juh against the Shyong-noo.
Jahng Chyen boldly volunteered and set out westward from Chahng-ahn with a retinue of over a hundred men.
As the mission passed through the Huh-shee Corridor, they were captured by Shyong-noo cavalry and taken to the Chahn-yoo's court.
The Chahn-yoo detained Jahng Chyen on the steppe, and the detention lasted for ten full years.
The Shyong-noo gave him a wife and children, trying to erode his will and make him forget his mission.
But Jahng Chyen never let go of the Han imperial envoy's staff, waiting for a chance to escape.
At last, during a Shyong-noo civil war, Jahng Chyen fled with his family and a few surviving followers.
He did not turn back east toward the Han, but continued westward, determined to fulfill his mission.
The mission passed through the kingdoms of Dah-ywen and Kahng-joo, finally reaching the court of the Greater Yweh-juh.
But the Yweh-juh people had settled comfortably in Bactria with abundant resources and no desire for revenge.
In the markets of Bactria, Jahng Chyen discovered Shu cloth and Chyong bamboo staffs from southwestern China.
He deduced that a trade route must exist connecting the southwestern regions to the Western Regions.
In 126 BC, after thirteen years of hardship, Jahng Chyen finally returned to Chahng-ahn.
Of the more than one hundred men who had set out, only he and his loyal companion Tahng-yee Foo survived.
The intelligence he brought back about the Western Regions was worth far more than any military alliance.

Episode 23: Opening of the Silk Road

After returning home, Jahng Chyen gave Emperor Wu a detailed report on the geography and customs of the Western Regions.
In 119 BC, Emperor Wu appointed Jahng Chyen as General of the Palace Gentlemen and sent him on a second westward mission.
The mission comprised three hundred men with ten thousand cattle and sheep and vast quantities of gold and silk, bound for the kingdom of Woo-swoon.
Jahng Chyen's objective was to form an alliance with the Woo-swoon to sever the Shyong-noo's right arm.
But the King of Woo-swoon feared the Shyong-noo's power and hesitated to commit to an alliance with the Han.
Nevertheless, he sent envoys to accompany Jahng Chyen back to Chahng-ahn to see the Han for themselves.
The Woo-swoon envoys were awed by the wealth and strength of the Han, and their kingdom thereafter aligned with the empire.
Deputy envoys dispatched by Jahng Chyen reached the kingdoms of Dah-ywen, Kahng-joo, Parthia, and Shun-doo.
Han envoys traveled as far as the Persian Empire and India, opening China's connections with the wider world.
Chinese silk and lacquerware flowed westward in an endless stream, becoming coveted luxuries among Western nobles.
Grapes, alfalfa, pomegranates, and sesame from the Western Regions entered China, enriching its agriculture and cuisine.
The Silk Road was officially opened, caravans traveled back and forth, and camel bells echoed across the desert sands.
Emperor Wu established four commanderies in the Huh-shee Corridor: Woo-way, Jahng-yeh, Jyoh-chwen, and Doon-hwahng.
West of Doon-hwahng, the Jade Gate Pass and the Yang Pass were built, becoming the strategic gateways of the Silk Road.
The Han settled colonists to farm the Huh-shee Corridor, ensuring the security of the Silk Road.
A great channel linking the civilizations of East and West stretched out between the deserts and the oases.

Episode 24: The Grace Decree and Centralization

Joo-foo Yen submitted a memorial to Emperor Wu proposing a brilliantly simple solution to dismantle the feudal kingdoms.
He suggested that feudal kings, instead of passing their entire realm to the eldest son alone, could divide their lands among all their sons.
The younger sons would be enfeoffed as marquises by the emperor, a seeming act of grace that actually carved the great kingdoms into small pieces.
Emperor Wu eagerly adopted the proposal, and it became known as the Grace Decree.
The Grace Decree cost not a single soldier or drop of blood, yet it fundamentally dissolved the power of the feudal lords.
The sons of feudal kings eagerly applied for their own fiefs, for every one of them wanted a share of the inheritance.
Within a few generations, the once-mighty kingdoms were fragmented into tiny, powerless territories.
King Lee-oh Ahn of Huainan and King Lee-oh Tsuh of Hengshan, unwilling to accept their fate, secretly plotted rebellion.
When Emperor Wu discovered the plot, he sent troops to suppress them, and both kings were forced to commit suicide.
The case implicated tens of thousands of people, and vast numbers of the kings' confidants and retainers were executed or exiled.
Emperor Wu established the position of regional inspector, dividing the empire into thirteen provinces for regular inspection.
The inspectors evaluated local officials on six specific criteria, creating a rigorous surveillance system over the provinces.
The palace court system was established, with the Imperial Secretary and palace attendants forming an inner court of close advisors.
The outer court, led by the chancellor, lost its real power, and decision-making shifted to the inner court.
Imperial authority was now concentrated to an unprecedented degree, making Emperor Wu one of the most powerful rulers in Chinese history.
The centralized state system of the Western Han reached a height never before seen.

Episode 25: Salt and Iron Monopolies and Economic Reforms

The price of salt in Chahng-ahn skyrocketed overnight, as wealthy merchants hoarded supplies and the common people suffered.
Sahng Hawng-yahng stood before the imperial court and presented a proposal that would reshape the empire's entire economic structure.
In 119 BC, Emperor Wu appointed Dongguo Shyen-yahng and Koong Jin as Grand Minister of Agriculture to oversee the salt and iron industries.
Sahng Hawng-yahng then implemented the policies of equitable transport and price stabilization, giving the state full control over commodity circulation.
Under the equitable transport system, local tribute goods were transported by government agencies, cutting out the profiteering middlemen.
The price stabilization system had the government buy when prices were low and sell when prices were high, smoothing market fluctuations.
Thirty-six salt offices and forty-eight iron offices were established across the empire, with all salt and iron production nationalized.
Anyone caught privately producing salt or iron would have an iron fetter clamped on their left foot and all their tools confiscated.
The salt and iron monopolies swelled the treasury, but they also fueled deep popular resentment against the government's economic stranglehold.
Emperor Wu then imposed the Swahn-min tax, a property levy on merchants at a crushing rate of six percent.
When merchants hid their wealth, the emperor issued the Gow-min decree, encouraging informants to report tax evaders.
Informants were rewarded with half of the confiscated property, and the empire descended into a frenzy of denunciation.
Most merchant families of middle-class standing or above were stripped of their wealth and ruined, their assets seized by the state.
The economic reforms were astonishingly effective, filling the treasury to overflowing and funding protracted imperial wars.
With this wealth, Emperor Wu launched year after year of campaigns against the Shyong-noo, Nahn-yweh, and Korea.
But the common people were bled dry, and hundreds of thousands of displaced peasants roamed the Gwahn-joong region, pushing social tensions to the breaking point.
Sahng Hawng-yahng's economic policies both enabled the splendor of Emperor Wu's reign and sowed the seeds of the empire's eventual decline.
The imperial fiscal machine ran at full speed, but it crushed the livelihoods and hopes of countless ordinary people beneath its wheels.

Episode 26: Conquest of Nahn-yweh and Min-yweh

The kingdom of Nahn-yweh was founded by Jow Two, a former Chin general, and had ruled Ling-nahn for five generations and over ninety years.
Jow Two, the former commandery governor of Nahn-hai under the Chin, carved out his own kingdom when the Chin Empire collapsed.
By the reign of Emperor Wu, King Jow Shing and his mother Queen Dowager Jiu wished to submit to the Han and become a vassal state.
But Chancellor Lyoo Jyah, a three-generation elder statesman of Nahn-yweh, was fiercely opposed to submitting to Han authority.
In 112 BC, Lyoo Jyah staged a coup, murdered King Jow Shing and Queen Dowager Jiu, and installed Jow Jyen-duh as the new king.
Emperor Wu was enraged and dispatched five armies under Loo Bwo-duh and Yahng Poo to crush the Nahn-yweh kingdom.
Admiral Yahng Poo led the naval fleet from Yoo-jahng, sailing down the Pearl River straight to the walls of Pahn-yoo.
General Loo Bwo-duh marched from Gway-yahng, crossing the Nanling Mountains by land to encircle Pahn-yoo with Yahng Poo's fleet.
In 111 BC, the Han army stormed Pahn-yoo, and Lyoo Jyah was captured and executed, ending the kingdom of Nahn-yweh.
The Han established nine commanderies on Nahn-yweh's former territory, from Nahn-hai to Dahn-ur on Hai-nahn Island.
That same year, King Yoo-shahn of Min-yweh rebelled against the Han, declaring himself emperor and attacking Han border posts.
Min-yweh was a kingdom descended from King Go-jyen of Yweh, occupying the region of modern-day Fujian.
The Han launched a joint land-and-sea assault, with Han Yweh leading the navy and Yahng Poo attacking from the interior.
The Min-yweh nobility turned against Yoo-shahn, killed him, and surrendered the entire kingdom to the Han.
Emperor Wu relocated the entire Min-yweh population to the region between the Yangtze and Huai rivers, and the kingdom of Min-yweh ceased to exist.
The Han Empire now stretched south to the South China Sea and east to the East China Sea, its territory vaster than ever before.

Episode 27: Opening the Southwest and Korea

While on a mission to Nahn-yweh, Tahng Mung discovered that Suh-chwahn pepper sauce was traded through Yeh-lahng to Pahn-yoo, and he proposed opening a route to the southwest.
Emperor Wu appointed Tahng Mung as General of the Palace Gentlemen, sending him with a thousand men and lavish gifts into the kingdom of Yeh-lahng.
The King of Yeh-lahng asked the Han envoy: Which is larger, the Han or my kingdom? This gave rise to the idiom of the conceited king of Yeh-lahng.
Yeh-lahng submitted to the Han, and the empire established the Chyahng-way Commandery, beginning its colonization of the southwestern tribes.
Suh-mah Shyahng-roo was dispatched to pacify the southwestern tribes, composing the Proclamation to Ba and Shu to reassure the local population.
The Han opened the Ling-gwahn Road, extending it westward to the Mo and Ruo rivers, building bridges and roads into the southwest.
Some southwestern tribes submitted while others resisted, and the Han army steadily advanced, establishing commanderies and counties.
In 109 BC, Emperor Wu dispatched Yahng Poo and Shoon Juh with combined naval and land forces to conquer Korea.
Korea was a kingdom founded by Way Mahn, a refugee from the state of Yan, with its capital near modern-day Pyongyang.
Yahng Poo sailed his fleet across the Bwo-hai Sea to land on the Korean coast, while Shoon Juh marched overland from Lyaow-doong.
The two armies besieged the capital Wahng-shyen for months, and internal strife led Korean ministers to kill King Way Yo-choo and surrender.
The Han established four commanderies on former Korean territory: Luh-lahng, Shwen-too, Lin-toon, and Jun-fahn.
By now, the Han Empire stretched from Korea in the east to Huh-shee in the west, from Jyaow-juh in the south to Shwo-fahng in the north.
Emperor Wu's expansionist policies made the Han the most vast empire in the known world at that time.

Episode 28: The Witchcraft Calamity – Part 1

Flashback: Chahng-ahn was ablaze, and Crown Prince Lee-oh Joo stood on the city gate, gazing at the burning palace. He drew his sword, but he already knew there was no way out.
In his later years, Emperor Wu was often ill and suspected that someone was using witchcraft to curse him, triggering a massive witch hunt in the palace.
Chancellor Goong-swoon Huh was implicated in a witchcraft case through his son's corruption, and his entire clan was exterminated.
The case also implicated Princess Yangshi and Princess Jooyi, both of whom were executed on Emperor Wu's orders.
Jyahng Choong was a cruel official favored by the aging emperor, and he had long nursed a grudge against Crown Prince Lee-oh Joo.
While Emperor Wu was recuperating at the Gahn-chwen Palace, Jyahng Choong falsely accused the Crown Prince of burying witchcraft dolls in his palace.
The Crown Prince could not defend himself, for his father was far away at Gahn-chwen, and Jyahng Choong had cut off all communication between them.
In desperation, the Crown Prince turned to his teacher Shuh Duh, who told him a story from history.
Shuh Duh said: When the First Emperor died, his heir Foo-soo commanded three hundred thousand troops, yet he surrendered meekly and was killed by forged edict.
Shuh Duh continued: If you do not resist, you are simply waiting for death, Prince Foo-soo's lesson is before your eyes.
Crown Prince Lee-oh Joo was moved by these words and resolved to fight back.
The Crown Prince issued a forged imperial edict, arrested Jyahng Choong, and personally executed him.
He released the prisoners of Chahng-ahn and armed them, preparing to resist the inevitable retaliation.

Episode 29: The Witchcraft Calamity – Part 2

Emperor Wu sent a messenger to summon the Crown Prince, but the messenger dared not enter the city and falsely reported that the Crown Prince had rebelled.
Emperor Wu flew into a rage and ordered Chancellor Lee-oh Choo-maow to raise troops and suppress the Crown Prince.
The Crown Prince freed Chahng-ahn's prisoners and armed them, fighting a desperate street battle against the Chancellor's forces.
The two sides fought for five days, the streets of Chahng-ahn ran with blood, and tens of thousands lay dead.
Rumors spread that the Crown Prince had truly rebelled, and his supporters dwindled by the hour.
The Crown Prince was defeated and fled through the southern gate of Chahng-ahn with his two sons.
In the palace, Empress Way Dzuh-foo surrendered her imperial seal and took her own life.
The Crown Prince fled to Hu County, where he hid in the home of a poor peasant family, living under an assumed name.
The authorities closed in relentlessly, and the Crown Prince's hiding place was discovered.
Crown Prince Lee-oh Joo hanged himself, and his two sons were killed alongside him.
The entire Way clan was exterminated, and the case implicated hundreds of thousands of people.
In the aftermath, Emperor Wu gradually came to understand the truth and was consumed by remorse.
He exterminated Jyahng Choong's entire clan and had the informant Su Wen burned alive on the Heng Bridge.
Emperor Wu built the Palace of Longing for the Heir and the Tower of Hoping for His Return, a monument to his grief.

Episode 30: The Loon-tai Edict of Self-Reproach

Flashback: The aging emperor sat alone in the vast, empty palace, with no empress, no crown prince, no Way Ching or Hwo Choo-bing by his side. He picked up his brush and began to write an edict.
After the witchcraft calamity, Emperor Wu sank into deep remorse and profound loneliness.
In 89 BC, Sahng Hawng-yahng proposed establishing military farming colonies at Loon-tai to continue the westward expansion.
Emperor Wu rejected Sahng Hawng-yahng's proposal and issued an edict that would echo through the ages.
This was the famous Loon-tai Edict, in which the emperor deeply expressed his regret for past transgressions.
In the edict, Emperor Wu declared that the first priority now was to stop arbitrary taxation and let the people devote themselves to farming.
The edict also proposed exempting horse breeders from corvée labor, to replenish the warhorses depleted by decades of campaigns.
Emperor Wu dismissed all the alchemists and sorcerers, admitting that he had been foolish and deceived by them.
He no longer pursued immortality and faced the approach of death with calm acceptance.
This was the first imperial edict in Chinese history in which an emperor publicly acknowledged his own mistakes.
In 87 BC, Emperor Wu passed away at the Wuzha Palace at the age of seventy.
On his deathbed, he named his eight-year-old son Lee-oh Foo-ling as the Crown Prince.
He appointed Hwo Gwahng, Jin Mee-dee, Shahng-gwahn Jyeh, and Sahng Hawng-yahng as regents to guide the young heir.
The great emperor departed from the world, leaving behind a vast empire and a profound reflection on the cost of his ambition.

Episode 31: Young Emperor Jow and Hwo Gwahng's Regency

In 87 BC, Emperor Wu passed away, and the eight-year-old Lee-oh Foo-ling ascended the throne as Emperor Jow of Han.
On his deathbed, Emperor Wu appointed Hwo Gwahng as the chief regent and Grand Marshal and General-in-Chief.
Hwo Gwahng was the younger half-brother of Hwo Choo-bing, a man of great composure who served for over twenty years without a single mistake.
Jin Mee-dee, the Chariot and Cavalry General, was a Shyong-noo prince who had become one of Emperor Wu's most trusted ministers.
Shahng-gwahn Jyeh and Sahng Hawng-yahng, the Left General and Imperial Secretary, were also appointed as regents alongside Hwo Gwahng and Jin Mee-dee.
In 86 BC, Jin Mee-dee died of illness, and the four regents became three.
Shahng-gwahn Jyeh and Sahng Hawng-yahng allied with King Lee-oh Dahn of Yan to plot against Hwo Gwahng.
They falsely accused Hwo Gwahng of using imperial regalia during a military inspection, a sign of treason.
The memorial was presented to the fourteen-year-old Emperor Jow, but the young emperor was unmoved.
Emperor Jow calmly said: Hwo Gwahng's inspection was recent, and King Yan is a thousand miles away, how could he possibly know?
The fourteen-year-old emperor had seen through the plot, and the entire court was stunned by his wisdom.
After his first scheme failed, Shahng-gwahn Jyeh plotted a coup with the Elder Princess Gai.
Hwo Gwahng struck first, executing Shahng-gwahn Jyeh, his sons, and all their followers.
The Elder Princess Gai and King Lee-oh Dahn of Yan were forced to commit suicide, and Hwo Gwahng now held absolute power over the empire.

Episode 32: The Salt and Iron Debate

Flashback: In the great hall of Way-yahng Palace, over sixty scholars and officials engaged in a fierce debate that shook the hall.
In 81 BC, Hwo Gwahng convened more than sixty worthy scholars and men of letters from across the empire to the capital.
They debated with Sahng Hawng-yahng over the salt and iron monopolies, the transport and price stabilization systems, and the liquor monopoly.
The scholars came from the common people, and they brought with them the deep resentment against the government's economic controls.
They argued that the salt and iron monopolies were the state stealing profits from the people and should be abolished.
They called for restoring free private enterprise and allowing the people to recover and prosper.
Sahng Hawng-yahng argued fiercely that the salt and iron revenues were the financial foundation of the war against the Shyong-noo.
He countered: Without the revenue from salt and iron, how would we feed and pay the soldiers on the frontier?
The debate lasted for months, with both sides exchanging arguments in increasingly heated tones.
The scholars invoked Confucian classics, while Sahng Hawng-yahng argued from the practical realities of state finance.
In the end, the liquor monopoly was abolished and some iron offices in the capital region were closed.
But the core salt and iron monopolies were preserved, and the foundations of state finance remained intact.
Hwahn Kwahn compiled the records of this debate into a book, which survives to this day as the Discourse on Salt and Iron.
In 80 BC, Sahng Hawng-yahng was implicated in Shahng-gwahn Jyeh's coup plot and was executed by Hwo Gwahng.
The economic genius who had managed the empire's finances for thirty years fell victim to a power struggle.
The Salt and Iron Debate was the first open policy debate convened by the imperial court in Chinese history.

Episode 33: Rise of Emperor Shooan

Flashback: In a prison in Chahng-ahn, a baby cried in the arms of a female convict, the great-grandson of Emperor Wu.
In 74 BC, Emperor Jow died at the age of twenty-one, leaving no heir to the throne.
Hwo Gwahng installed Lee-oh Huh, the King of Chahng-yee, as emperor, but his behavior shocked everyone.
In just twenty-seven days on the throne, Lee-oh Huh committed over a thousand acts of absurd misconduct.
Hwo Gwahng consulted with the ministers and had Lee-oh Huh deposed in the name of the Empress Dowager.
The Han Dynasty needed a new emperor, and someone mentioned the name Lee-oh Beengyi.
Lee-oh Beengyi was the great-grandson of Emperor Wu, grandson of Crown Prince Lee-oh Joo, and he was eighteen years old, living among the common people.
During the Witchcraft Calamity, Crown Prince Lee-oh Joo's entire family was killed, and only this infant survived.
The prison warden Bing Jee risked his life to hide the baby in the cell of a female convict who nursed him.
Later, Bing Jee sent the boy to live with his grandmother's family, the Shuh clan, where he grew up among ordinary people.
Lee-oh Beengyi married a commoner woman named Shoo Ping-joon and lived a simple life.
Growing up among the common people, he understood their suffering and saw through the corruption of local officials.
Hwo Gwahng sent envoys to escort Lee-oh Beengyi to the palace, and the young man did not know what awaited him.
In 74 BC, Lee-oh Beengyi was formally enthroned and changed his name to Lee-oh Shoon, becoming Emperor Shooan of Han.
He knelt in the ancestral temple before the spirit tablet of the founding emperor, his heart filled with emotion.

Episode 34: Emperor Shooan's Revival

In 68 BC, Hwo Gwahng died, and Emperor Shooan finally took personal control of the empire after years of waiting.
He buried Hwo Gwahng with imperial honors, but he knew the Huo clan's power had to be dismantled.
Hwo Gwahng's wife, Hwo Shyen, had bribed the imperial physician to poison Emperor Shooan's beloved first wife, Empress Shoo.
Shoo Ping-joon was his wife from his commoner days, and she was the love of his life.
When Emperor Shooan learned the truth, he was consumed by grief and rage, but he chose to bide his time.
He gradually removed the Huo clan members from key positions and replaced them with his own trusted men.
Two years later, when the Huo clan plotted rebellion, Emperor Shooan finally had the chance he had been waiting for.
He ordered the entire Huo clan exterminated, sparing only Hwo Gwahng himself who was already dead.
Emperor Shooan reformed the bureaucracy, requiring senior officials to serve long terms and be promoted on merit.
He often said: The people suffer because corrupt and cruel officials enforce unjust policies.
If local officials were incompetent, the common people could report them directly to the central government.
He reduced taxes and established state granaries across the empire to stabilize food prices.
The granaries bought grain when prices were low and sold when prices were high, protecting both farmers and consumers.
Under Emperor Shooan, the Protectorate of the Western Regions was formally established, extending Han control over the Tarim Basin.
The Shyong-noo empire split into northern and southern factions, and the Southern Shyong-noo submitted to the Han.
This era was known as the Revival under Emperor Shooan, and the Han Dynasty reached the height of its power.

Episode 35: Shyong-noo Submission and Chun Tahng's Victory

In 51 BC, the Shyong-noo Chahn-yoo Hoo-hahn-yeh traveled to Chahng-ahn to pay homage to Emperor Shooan of Han.
The Shyong-noo Chahn-yoo formally submitted to the Han emperor, a historic moment unprecedented in fifty years of war.
Emperor Shooan received Hoo-hahn-yeh at the Gahn-chwen Palace with ceremonies surpassing those for the feudal kings.
For the first time, the Han emperor and the Shyong-noo Chahn-yoo met as sovereign and vassal.
The Shyong-noo split into northern and southern factions, with the south submitting to the Han and the north retreating westward.
Emperor Shooan commissioned portraits of eleven meritorious officials in the Chee-lin Pavilion to honor their service.
In the Chee-lin Pavilion, Hwo Gwahng was listed first, but his name was not written, only his title and the character Huo.
Emperor Shooan thus acknowledged Hwo Gwahng's achievements while also marking his clan's crime of treason.
In 36 BC, the Northern Shyong-noo Chahn-yoo Juh-juh had settled in the kingdom of Kahng-joo and was killing Han envoys.
Gahn Yen-sho, the Protector General of the Western Regions, and his deputy Chun Tahng, resolved to take military action.
Chun Tahng told Gahn Yen-sho: If we wait for the court's approval, we will lose our chance.
Chun Tahng forged an imperial edict and mobilized the armies of the Western Regions, assembling a force of forty thousand men.
The Han army marched swiftly across the desert and launched a surprise assault on the Chahn-yoo's capital.
The Han forces breached the city walls and killed the Juh-juh Chahn-yoo in the chaos of battle.
In his memorial to Emperor Shooan, Chun Tahng wrote: Those who dare offend the mighty Han, however far away, shall be put to death!
These words have echoed through the ages, becoming the ultimate expression of the Chinese spirit of defiance.
The remnants of the Northern Shyong-noo fled westward, gradually vanishing into the dust of history.

Episode 36: Protectorate of the Western Regions

In 60 BC, Emperor Shooan appointed Jung Jee as the first Protector General of the Western Regions, with headquarters at Woo-lay City.
The Han Dynasty formally incorporated the Western Regions into its territory, extending its rule across the Tyen-shahn Mountains.
Jung Jee was the first Protector General, overseeing the thirty-six kingdoms of the Western Regions and mediating their disputes.
He traveled across the Western Regions, pacifying the kingdoms and ensuring the Silk Road remained open and secure.
The Protector General's duties included supervising the kingdoms, mediating conflicts, and protecting merchants and travelers.
He was also tasked with defending against Shyong-noo remnants and maintaining peace and stability in the Western Regions.
The Han stationed garrison troops to farm the land, turning places like Choo-lee and Loon-tai into granaries for the Han army.
The soldier-farmers defended the frontier while cultivating the land, introducing advanced farming techniques to the Western Regions.
Han institutions, culture, and technology spread steadily westward along the Silk Road.
The kingdoms of the Western Regions sent envoys to the Han court, and the ties between the heartland and the frontier grew ever stronger.
From Jahng Chyen's first journey to the establishment of the Protectorate, the Han spent nearly eighty years on this endeavor.
In those eighty years, countless Han soldiers buried their bones in the desert, finally completing the conquest and administration of the Western Regions.

Episode 37: Decline under Emperor Ywen and Shuh Shyen's Power

In 49 BC, Emperor Shooan passed away, and Crown Prince Lee-oh Shuh ascended the throne as Emperor Ywen of Han.
On his deathbed, Emperor Shooan had sighed: The one who will bring chaos to my house is the Crown Prince.
Emperor Ywen was a Confucian idealist who promoted benevolent rule and abolished many harsh punishments.
But he was indecisive and weak-willed, lacking the resolve needed to govern a complex empire.
The eunuch Shuh Shyen, skilled in law and administration, gained Emperor Ywen's trust and seized power.
Shuh Shyen purged his rivals, built a faction of loyalists, and removed every honest official from the court.
He framed the former general Shyaow Wahng-juh, accusing the venerable statesman of plotting treason.
Shyaow Wahng-juh was Emperor Ywen's teacher, a renowned Confucian scholar respected throughout the empire.
Shuh Shyen falsely accused him of rebellion, and Emperor Ywen agreed to have him imprisoned and interrogated.
Shyaow Wahng-juh, refusing to suffer humiliation, drank poison in his cell and died to preserve his honor.
Emperor Ywen wept bitterly upon hearing of his death, yet he never held Shuh Shyen accountable.
Instead, he continued to entrust Shuh Shyen with power, exposing his own weakness for all to see.
From Emperor Ywen onward, the center of political power shifted from the emperor to consort clans and eunuchs.
The empire began its irreversible decline, and the glory of the golden age slowly faded into shadow.

Episode 38: Wahng Jaow-joon's Marriage Beyond the Frontier

Flashback: On the road outside Chahng-ahn, a procession of carriages journeyed slowly northward, kicking up clouds of yellow dust.
A woman in the carriage lifted the curtain to gaze back at Chahng-ahn, knowing she would never return.
In 33 BC, the Shyong-noo Chahn-yoo Hoo-hahn-yeh came to the Han court for the third time, requesting a marriage alliance.
Emperor Ywen gave Wahng Jaow-joon, a palace lady, to the Chahn-yoo as his queen, and she set out for the frontier.
Legend says Wahng Jaow-joon never met the emperor, because the painter Maow Yen-sho made her portrait ugly when she refused to bribe him.
Only at her departure did Emperor Ywen see her beauty, and he was filled with regret but could not undo his decision.
After arriving among the Shyong-noo, Wahng Jaow-joon was given the title Ning-hoo Yen-juh, meaning the Queen Who Brings Peace.
She taught the Shyong-noo people to weave cloth and farm the land, spreading Han culture and earning their deep affection.
After Hoo-hahn-yeh Chahn-yoo's death, Wahng Jaow-joon sent a letter to the Han court, asking to return to her homeland.
But Emperor Chung ordered her to follow Shyong-noo custom and marry Hoo-hahn-yeh's son, remaining on the steppe.
She lived among the Shyong-noo for decades and was buried on the steppe, her Green Tomb remembered through the ages.
Wahng Jaow-joon's marriage became a symbol of peace between Han and Shyong-noo, and for over sixty years the northern border saw no major war.

Episode 39: Rise of the Wahng Clan

Empress Wahng Jung-joon, the wife of Emperor Ywen, lived through the reigns of four emperors, an exceptionally long life.
She was one of the longest-serving empress dowagers in Chinese history, wielding influence for over sixty years.
When Emperor Chung ascended the throne, Wahng Jung-joon became empress dowager, and the Wahng clan's power exploded.
Her brothers Wahng Fung, Wahng Shahng, Wahng Gun, and Wahng Yin all served as Grand Marshal and General-in-Chief in succession.
Nine members of the Wahng clan were enfeoffed as marquises, and five served as Grand Marshal, dominating every key post.
The power of the consort clan reached its peak, and the empire knew the Wahng name better than the Liu name.
Emperor Chung was infatuated with the sisters Jow Fay-yen and Jow Huh-duh, drowning in wine and women while neglecting the state.
Jow Fay-yen framed every pregnant consort in the palace, ensuring Emperor Chung died without an heir and creating a succession crisis.
After Emperor Chung's sudden death, Emperor Ai took the throne, but he was obsessed with his male favorite Doong Shyen.
Emperor Ai once even considered abdicating the throne to Doong Shyen, shocking every minister in the court.
Emperor Ai died young, and Emperor Ping ascended the throne at just nine years old, leaving the court in the hands of consort relatives.
Wahng Jung-joon's nephew Wahng Mahng was appointed Grand Marshal, beginning his final ascent to supreme power.

Episode 40: Wahng Mahng's Usurpation

Flashback: In 8 AD, in the front hall of Way-yahng Palace, a fifty-three-year-old man stood before the dragon throne.
He removed his Han court robes and put on the imperial crown, his name was Wahng Mahng, emperor of the Sheen Dynasty.
Wahng Mahng was Wahng Jung-joon's nephew, known in his youth for humility and frugality, hailed by the court as a living sage.
He gave away his wealth to help the poor, practiced self-restraint, and won the admiration of scholars and commoners alike.
He first installed the two-year-old Lee-oh Ying as crown prince and declared himself Acting Emperor, slowly hollowing out the Han house.
In 8 AD, believing the time was ripe, he forced his aunt Wahng Jung-joon to hand over the imperial heirloom seal.
Wahng Jung-joon cursed Wahng Mahng as an ungrateful wolf and hurled the seal to the ground in fury.
The heirloom seal was chipped at one corner, and Wahng Mahng repaired it with gold.
Wahng Mahng established the Sheen Dynasty, launching reforms to restore the well-field system, abolish slavery, and reform the currency.
But his policies were detached from reality, constantly changing, and they plunged the empire into chaos and suffering.
In 9 AD, the Western Han Dynasty officially fell, having lasted twelve emperors since Lee-oh Bahng's coronation at Ding-taow.
The Western Han endured for two hundred and ten years, leaving a legacy of centralized governance, Confucian orthodoxy, and the Silk Road.
The Han Chinese identity was forged during this era, shaping the next two thousand years of Chinese civilization.
But the story of the empire was not over, for twenty years later a descendant of the Liu clan would raise the Han banner once more.
His legend was just beginning, and the dawn of the Eastern Han revival was about to illuminate the shattered land.
