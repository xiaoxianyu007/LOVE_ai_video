Episode 1: Pearl Harbor and Japan's Southern Expansion

In the years leading up to 1941, Japan had been waging a brutal war of conquest in China. The empire sought natural resources, especially oil, rubber, and metals, to fuel its industrial and military expansion. When the United States imposed trade embargoes and froze Japanese assets in response to the invasion of French Indochina, Japan's leaders faced a choice. They could either abandon their imperial ambitions or strike at the source of the embargo. They chose war.

On the morning of December 7, 1941, Japanese aircraft carriers launched a surprise attack on the US Pacific Fleet anchored at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii. The attack came in two waves, with over three hundred and fifty aircraft striking the harbor. Eight American battleships were damaged or sunk, including the USS Arizona, which exploded and sank with over a thousand sailors trapped inside. Over two thousand four hundred Americans were killed in the attack. The following day, the United States declared war on Japan.

The Japanese attack was not an isolated event. Within hours, Japanese forces struck at American bases in the Philippines, Guam, and Wake Island, and at British possessions in Hong Kong and Malaya. The Japanese invasion of the Philippines began on December 8, 1941, with landings on Luzon. General Douglas MacArthur commanded the defense, but his forces were quickly overwhelmed by the Japanese advance. Manila fell in January 1942, and the defenders retreated to the Bataan Peninsula and Corregidor Island.

The fall of Singapore in February 1942 was one of the greatest military disasters in British history. The fortress city, considered impregnable, surrendered to a Japanese force that was outnumbered but had outmaneuvered the defenders by attacking through the supposedly impassable Malayan jungle. Prime Minister Winston Churchill called it the worst disaster in British military history. Over eighty thousand British, Australian, and Indian troops were marched into captivity, many to face years of brutal treatment as prisoners of war.

By mid-1942, Japan had created a vast empire stretching from Burma to the Gilbert Islands, from the Aleutians to the Solomon Islands. The Rising Sun flag flew over an area that encompassed a significant portion of the globe. The Japanese had achieved their objectives with stunning speed, but they had also awakened a sleeping giant. The United States was now fully committed to total war, and its industrial might would soon be brought to bear on the Pacific theater with devastating effect.

Episode 2: Midway — The Tide Turns

In the spring of 1942, Japan sought to complete its destruction of the US Pacific Fleet. Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto, the architect of the Pearl Harbor attack, planned to lure the remaining American carriers into a decisive battle at Midway Atoll, a tiny island roughly a thousand miles northwest of Hawaii. He assembled the largest naval force ever seen in the Pacific, with four fleet carriers, seven battleships, and hundreds of support vessels. The Japanese were confident of victory.

But the Americans had a secret weapon. Navy codebreakers at Station HYPO in Hawaii had partially cracked the Japanese naval code, JN-25. They knew that an attack on a target designated AF was imminent, and they correctly deduced that AF was Midway. The commander of the US Pacific Fleet, Admiral Chester Nimitz, quietly positioned his three aircraft carriers, Enterprise, Hornet, and Yorktown, northeast of Midway, waiting for the Japanese to arrive. The trap was set.

The battle began on June 4, 1942, when Japanese aircraft bombed Midway Island. American land-based aircraft attacked the Japanese carriers but inflicted little damage. Then, at the critical moment, American dive-bombers from the Enterprise and Yorktown found the Japanese carriers while their planes were being refueled and rearmed on the decks. The bombs struck the carriers Akagi, Kaga, and Soryu simultaneously, turning them into infernos within minutes. The fourth carrier, Hiryu, was sunk later that day.

In a single morning, Japan lost four fleet carriers, over two hundred and fifty aircraft, and many of its most experienced pilots. The Americans lost the carrier Yorktown and one destroyer. The Battle of Midway was a decisive American victory that changed the course of the Pacific War. Japan's offensive capability was crippled, and the strategic initiative passed permanently to the United States. The Japanese navy would never recover from the loss of its carriers and aircrews.

The victory at Midway was followed by the first Allied offensive in the Pacific. In August 1942, US Marines landed on Guadalcanal in the Solomon Islands, beginning the first American amphibious campaign of the war. The battle for Guadalcanal raged for six months, with brutal fighting on land, at sea, and in the air. Japanese soldiers, fighting with fanatical determination, defended the island to the death. The Americans finally secured Guadalcanal in February 1943, proving that the Japanese could be defeated in ground combat.

Episode 3: Island Hopping — From Tarawa to the Philippines

After Guadalcanal, the Allies adopted a strategy of island hopping, bypassing heavily fortified Japanese positions and seizing strategically important islands to serve as bases for the next advance. The objective was to reach the Philippines and then Japan itself. Admiral Nimitz led the advance through the Central Pacific, while General MacArthur pushed northward from New Guinea. The two-pronged offensive kept the Japanese off balance and stretched their defensive perimeter.

The first major test of island hopping came at Tarawa in November 1943. The tiny atoll of Betio was heavily fortified, and the Japanese commander had boasted that a million men could not take it in a hundred years. The US Marines took it in seventy-six hours, but at a terrible cost. Over a thousand Marines were killed and more than two thousand wounded. The battle shocked the American public and led to significant improvements in amphibious tactics for future landings.

The Marianas campaign in mid-1944 brought the war closer to Japan. The islands of Saipan, Guam, and Tinian were captured after fierce fighting. The Battle of the Philippine Sea, fought in June 1944 during the Saipan invasion, was one of the largest carrier battles in history. American pilots called it the Great Marianas Turkey Shoot, as they shot down nearly three hundred Japanese aircraft while losing only thirty of their own. The battle destroyed what remained of Japan's carrier aviation.

The capture of the Marianas was significant for another reason. For the first time, Japan's home islands were within range of the new American B-29 Superfortress bombers. From the airfields on Saipan and Tinian, the strategic bombing of Japan began. The American bombers would eventually drop thousands of tons of incendiaries on Japanese cities, culminating in the firebombing of Tokyo in March 1945, which killed over one hundred thousand people in a single night, the deadliest air raid in history.

In October 1944, General MacArthur made good on his famous promise to return to the Philippines. US forces landed on Leyte Island, and the largest naval battle in history erupted in Leyte Gulf. The Japanese threw everything they had left into the battle, including their remaining battleships and the first organized use of kamikaze suicide attacks. The battle was a decisive American victory, destroying the Japanese navy as an effective fighting force. The Philippines was liberated by early 1945, at the cost of over sixty thousand American casualties.

Episode 4: Iwo Jima and Okinawa

As American forces closed in on Japan, the fighting became increasingly desperate and bloody. The Japanese high command had adopted a strategy of attrition, hoping to inflict such heavy casualties on the advancing Americans that they would negotiate a peace rather than invade the home islands. Every island became a fortress, every cave a strongpoint, and every Japanese soldier was expected to fight to the death. The campaigns at Iwo Jima and Okinawa would test this strategy to its limits.

Iwo Jima, a volcanic island just six hundred and sixty miles from Tokyo, was defended by over twenty thousand Japanese soldiers under General Tadamichi Kuribayashi. He had constructed an elaborate network of tunnels, bunkers, and underground positions that made the island virtually impregnable. When the Marines landed on February 19, 1945, they found themselves in a nightmare. The Japanese held their fire until the beaches were crowded with men and equipment, then opened up with devastating effect.

The battle for Iwo Jima lasted thirty-six days and cost over six thousand eight hundred American lives. The Marines fought yard by yard across the island, using flamethrowers and explosives to clear the Japanese from their underground positions. The iconic photograph of Marines raising the American flag on Mount Suribachi became one of the most famous images of the war. The capture of Iwo Jima provided a vital emergency landing strip for B-29 bombers returning from missions over Japan, saving thousands of aircrew lives.

Okinawa, the last major battle of the Pacific War, was even more horrific. The island, just three hundred and forty miles from the Japanese mainland, was defended by over one hundred thousand Japanese soldiers. American forces began landing on April 1, 1945, and met little initial resistance. But as they moved inland, they encountered the main Japanese defensive line, the Shuri Line, which stretched across the island's narrow waist. The fighting that followed was some of the most intense of the entire war.

The battle for Okinawa raged for eighty-two days. Japanese soldiers launched repeated massed counterattacks, often at night, fighting with a ferocity that stunned their American opponents. The Japanese also unleashed waves of kamikaze attacks on the American fleet offshore, sinking dozens of ships and killing thousands of sailors. By the time the island was secured in June 1945, over twelve thousand Americans and over one hundred thousand Japanese soldiers had been killed. The civilians of Okinawa suffered terribly, with an estimated one hundred and fifty thousand non-combatants killed. The battle demonstrated that an invasion of the Japanese home islands would be a bloodbath of unimaginable proportions.

Episode 5: The Atomic Bombs and the Surrender

By the summer of 1945, Japan was a nation in ruins. Its cities were being systematically destroyed by American bombing, its navy was at the bottom of the sea, and its armies were isolated and starving across the Pacific. Yet the Japanese military leadership refused to surrender. They prepared for a final, apocalyptic battle on the home islands, arming civilians with bamboo spears and training them to fight the expected American invasion. The Allied planners estimated that an invasion of Japan could cost over a million American casualties.

Unknown to the Japanese, the United States had been secretly developing a weapon of unprecedented destructive power. The Manhattan Project, a massive research and industrial effort involving over one hundred and thirty thousand people, had produced the first atomic bombs. On July 16, 1945, the first atomic bomb was successfully tested in the New Mexico desert. President Harry Truman, who had only learned of the project upon taking office, now faced the most momentous decision of the war.

On August 6, 1945, the B-29 bomber Enola Gay dropped an atomic bomb nicknamed Little Boy on the city of Hiroshima. The bomb detonated above the city with the force of over fifteen thousand tons of TNT, instantly killing an estimated eighty thousand people and destroying the city in a flash of heat and light. Three days later, on August 9, a second atomic bomb, Fat Man, was dropped on the city of Nagasaki, killing another forty thousand people instantly. The devastation was beyond anything the world had ever seen.

The Soviet Union declared war on Japan on August 8, invading Manchuria with a massive force of over one million soldiers. The Japanese army in Manchuria, once the pride of the empire, collapsed within days. Faced with atomic destruction, Soviet invasion, and the complete destruction of their nation, the Japanese government finally made the decision to surrender. Emperor Hirohito, in a recorded radio address broadcast on August 15, 1945, announced to the Japanese people that the war was over.

On September 2, 1945, aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay, Japanese officials signed the formal instrument of surrender. General Douglas MacArthur, Supreme Commander of the Allied Powers, presided over the ceremony. The Second World War was finally over, six years and one day after it had begun. In the Pacific, as in Europe, the cost had been staggering. Over twenty-five million people, combatants and civilians alike, had been killed in the Asia-Pacific theater. The world had entered the atomic age, and the Cold War was already beginning.
