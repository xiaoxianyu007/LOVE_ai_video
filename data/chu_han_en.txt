Episode 1: The Feast at Hong Gate

In 206 BC, the Chin Empire collapsed and two warlords raced to claim the imperial capital Shyen-yahng. Lee-oh Bahng, a commoner turned rebel leader, entered the Chin heartland first and accepted the surrender of the last Chin emperor. But Shyahng Yoo, the most fearsome general of the age, arrived with an army of four hundred thousand men camped at Hong Gate. A traitor named Tsao Woo-shahng informed Shyahng Yoo that Lee-oh Bahng intended to proclaim himself king of all Gwahn-joong. Shyahng Yoo's chief advisor Fahn Dzung urged him to destroy Lee-oh Bahng immediately before he grew too powerful. That night, Shyahng Yoo's uncle Shyahng Bwo secretly rode to Lee-oh Bahng's camp to warn his friend Jahng Lee-ahng of the impending attack. Jahng Lee-ahng convinced Lee-oh Bahng to humble himself and seek Shyahng Yoo's forgiveness in person. The next morning, Lee-oh Bahng arrived at Hong Gate with only a hundred horsemen and knelt before the Hegemon-King. At the banquet, Fahn Dzung raised his jade pendant three times as a signal to kill Lee-oh Bahng, but Shyahng Yoo hesitated and did nothing. Desperate, Fahn Dzung ordered Shyahng Jwahng to perform a sword dance and strike Lee-oh Bahng during the performance. Shyahng Bwo rose to dance alongside him, shielding Lee-oh Bahng with his own body. Jahng Lee-ahng slipped outside and summoned the fearsome warrior Fahn Kwai, who burst into the tent brandishing his sword and shield. Fahn Kwai's raw courage so impressed Shyahng Yoo that the Hegemon-King forgot his murderous intent. Seizing the moment, Lee-oh Bahng excused himself to the latrine and fled back to his camp, leaving Jahng Lee-ahng behind to present gifts. Fahn Dzung smashed the jade gifts with his sword and cried out that the fool boy was not worth advising. Shyahng Yoo later entered Shyen-yahng, executed the surrendered Chin emperor, and burned the magnificent Chin palaces to the ground. But by sparing Lee-oh Bahng at Hong Gate, the Hegemon-King had sealed his own doom.

Episode 2: Secretly Crossing Chun-tsahng

In the spring of 206 BC, Shyahng Yoo divided the empire into eighteen kingdoms and proclaimed himself Hegemon-King of Western Choo. He exiled Lee-oh Bahng to the remote and mountainous region of Hahn-joong, far from the fertile heartland of Gwahn-joong. To block Lee-oh Bahng's return, Shyahng Yoo placed three former Chin generals known as the Three Chins in charge of guarding the passes. Lee-oh Bahng, following the advice of his strategist Jahng Lee-ahng, burned the wooden plank roads behind him as he marched into Hahn-joong. This gesture convinced Shyahng Yoo that Lee-oh Bahng had no intention of ever returning east. But in Hahn-joong, a military genius was about to change the course of history. Shyaow Huh, Lee-oh Bahng's chancellor, recognized the extraordinary talent of a low-ranking officer named Hahn Sheen. Under the moonlight, Shyaow Huh chased after the departing Hahn Sheen and persuaded him to return, then urged Lee-oh Bahng to appoint him as supreme commander. Lee-oh Bahng built a grand altar and formally invested Hahn Sheen as Grand General in a solemn ceremony. Hahn Sheen unveiled his master plan: outwardly repair the burned plank roads to deceive the Three Chins, while secretly leading the main army through the ancient mountain path to Chun-tsahng. In the eighth month of 206 BC, Hahn Sheen's army burst out of the mountains and fell upon the unprepared forces of Jahng Hahn. The Chin veteran Jahng Hahn fought desperately but was pushed back to the fortress of Fay-chyoh. Suh-mah Sheen and Doong Yee, the other two Chin kings, surrendered without a fight. Within months, Lee-oh Bahng had reconquered the entire Gwahn-joong region, the old Chin heartland. With the fertile Gwahn-joong plain as his base and Shyaow Huh managing logistics and recruitment, Lee-oh Bahng was now ready to challenge Shyahng Yoo for the empire.

Episode 3: The Pung-chung Disaster

In the spring of 205 BC, Lee-oh Bahng assembled a massive coalition army of five hundred and sixty thousand soldiers from allied kingdoms. Shyahng Yoo was away in the eastern state of Chee, brutally suppressing a rebellion and leaving his capital Pung-chung virtually undefended. Lee-oh Bahng's coalition swept into Pung-chung and captured the city with ease. Drunk on victory, Lee-oh Bahng seized Shyahng Yoo's treasures and women and held days of celebration, neglecting all military precautions. But Shyahng Yoo was not an ordinary general. Upon hearing that his capital had fallen, he left his main army in Chee and personally led thirty thousand elite cavalry on a lightning march back to Pung-chung. Shyahng Yoo's cavalry struck at dawn from the west, a direction the Han army never expected. The surprised Han forces collapsed in chaos, with tens of thousands slaughtered in the narrow streets of Pung-chung. The rout continued all the way to the Sway River, where countless fleeing soldiers drowned in the waters. Lee-oh Bahng's father and his wife Lyoo Juh were captured by Choo forces and would remain prisoners for years. Lee-oh Bahng himself fled with only a handful of horsemen, and in his desperate flight he even pushed his own children out of the carriage to lighten the load. Only the loyal charioteer Shyah-ho Ying saved the children by repeatedly pulling them back onto the vehicle. The Pung-chung catastrophe shattered Lee-oh Bahng's coalition and sent the Han forces reeling back to the fortified city of Shing-yahng. It was the most humiliating defeat of Lee-oh Bahng's entire career, and the survivors who regrouped at Shing-yahng knew that the war was far from over.

Episode 4: The Battle of Jing-shing

In the autumn of 205 BC, while Lee-oh Bahng held the line at Shing-yahng against Shyahng Yoo's relentless assaults, Hahn Sheen marched north to open a second front. He first conquered the kingdom of Way, capturing its king Way Bao, and then swept through the state of Dai with astonishing speed. In the winter of 204 BC, Hahn Sheen led thirty thousand troops into the narrow Jing-shing Pass, where a Jow army of two hundred thousand awaited him. The Jow general Chun Yoo rejected the wise counsel of his advisor Lee Dzwo-chuh, who had proposed cutting Hahn Sheen's supply lines. Confident in his overwhelming numbers, Chun Yoo prepared to crush Hahn Sheen in a single decisive battle. Hahn Sheen did something that violated every military principle known to the ancients. He arrayed his troops with their backs to the Myen-mahn River, leaving no line of retreat. As dawn broke, Hahn Sheen's main force engaged the Jow army, then feigned a retreat toward the river. The Jow soldiers, believing victory was at hand, abandoned their fortifications to pursue the fleeing Han troops. But Hahn Sheen had hidden two thousand cavalry behind the Jow lines, and they seized the empty Jow camp and replaced all the Jow banners with the red flags of Han. When the Jow troops turned back and saw their camp filled with Han flags, they believed they were surrounded and panicked. The trapped Jow army was annihilated, and Chun Yoo was killed on the battlefield. Hahn Sheen treated the captured Lee Dzwo-chuh with great respect and adopted his strategy for the subsequent conquest of Yan. Within a year, Hahn Sheen had conquered Jow, Yan, and Chee, stripping Shyahng Yoo of all his northern allies.

Episode 5: The Tragedy of Gai-shee-ah

By the autumn of 203 BC, four years of war had exhausted both sides and Shyahng Yoo agreed to divide China along the Hawng Canal. But the moment Shyahng Yoo withdrew eastward, Lee-oh Bahng's strategists Jahng Lee-ahng and Chun Ping urged him to break the treaty and strike while the Choo army was retreating. Lee-oh Bahng attacked, but Shyahng Yoo turned and defeated him at Goo-ling, and the Han forces had to wait for reinforcements. In the winter of 202 BC, Lee-oh Bahng promised Hahn Sheen the title of King of Chee and Pung Yweh the title of King of Liang. With their armies now committed, a massive Han force of six hundred thousand men converged on Shyahng Yoo's remaining one hundred thousand troops at Gai-shee-ah. Hahn Sheen, commanding the great encirclement, ordered his soldiers to sing the folk songs of Choo throughout the night. Hearing their homeland melodies echoing from all directions, the homesick Choo soldiers believed that Han had already conquered all of Choo. Their morale shattered, and thousands deserted under the cover of darkness. In his tent, Shyahng Yoo gazed at his beloved concubine Yoo Jee and sang a poem of despair: my strength could pull mountains, my spirit dominated the world, yet the times are against me and my horse will not run. Yoo Jee drew a sword and took her own life rather than burden her king in his final battle. Shyahng Yoo led eight hundred elite horsemen in a desperate breakout charge and cut through the Han siege lines. Pursued by thousands, he was led astray by a farmer and lost his way, and by the time he reached the Woo-jyahng River only twenty-eight riders remained. A ferryman offered to take Shyahng Yoo across the river to safety in his homeland of Jyahng-doong. But Shyahng Yoo refused, saying that he could not face the elders of Jyahng-doong after leading eight thousand of their sons to their deaths. He gave his horse to the ferryman, then turned to fight on foot, killing hundreds of Han soldiers single-handedly. Severely wounded, Shyahng Yoo recognized an old acquaintance among the Han ranks and declared that he would grant him the reward for his head. The Hegemon-King of Western Choo then drew his sword and cut his own throat, ending the Choo-Han Contention at the age of thirty. With Shyahng Yoo dead, Lee-oh Bahng unified China and founded the Han Dynasty, a civilization that would endure for four centuries and give its name to the Chinese people themselves.
