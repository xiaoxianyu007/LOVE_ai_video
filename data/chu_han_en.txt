Episode 1: The Feast at Hong Gate

In 206 BC, the Qin Empire collapsed and two warlords raced to claim the imperial capital Xianyang. Liu Bang, a commoner turned rebel leader, entered the Qin heartland first and accepted the surrender of the last Qin emperor. But Xiang Yu, the most fearsome general of the age, arrived with an army of four hundred thousand men camped at Hong Gate. A traitor named Cao Wushang informed Xiang Yu that Liu Bang intended to proclaim himself king of all Guanzhong. Xiang Yu's chief advisor Fan Zeng urged him to destroy Liu Bang immediately before he grew too powerful. That night, Xiang Yu's uncle Xiang Bo secretly rode to Liu Bang's camp to warn his friend Zhang Liang of the impending attack. Zhang Liang convinced Liu Bang to humble himself and seek Xiang Yu's forgiveness in person. The next morning, Liu Bang arrived at Hong Gate with only a hundred horsemen and knelt before the Hegemon-King. At the banquet, Fan Zeng raised his jade pendant three times as a signal to kill Liu Bang, but Xiang Yu hesitated and did nothing. Desperate, Fan Zeng ordered Xiang Zhuang to perform a sword dance and strike Liu Bang during the performance. Xiang Bo rose to dance alongside him, shielding Liu Bang with his own body. Zhang Liang slipped outside and summoned the fearsome warrior Fan Kuai, who burst into the tent brandishing his sword and shield. Fan Kuai's raw courage so impressed Xiang Yu that the Hegemon-King forgot his murderous intent. Seizing the moment, Liu Bang excused himself to the latrine and fled back to his camp, leaving Zhang Liang behind to present gifts. Fan Zeng smashed the jade gifts with his sword and cried out that the fool boy was not worth advising. Xiang Yu later entered Xianyang, executed the surrendered Qin emperor, and burned the magnificent Qin palaces to the ground. But by sparing Liu Bang at Hong Gate, the Hegemon-King had sealed his own doom.

Episode 2: Secretly Crossing Chencang

In the spring of 206 BC, Xiang Yu divided the empire into eighteen kingdoms and proclaimed himself Hegemon-King of Western Chu. He exiled Liu Bang to the remote and mountainous region of Hanzhong, far from the fertile heartland of Guanzhong. To block Liu Bang's return, Xiang Yu placed three former Qin generals known as the Three Qins in charge of guarding the passes. Liu Bang, following the advice of his strategist Zhang Liang, burned the wooden plank roads behind him as he marched into Hanzhong. This gesture convinced Xiang Yu that Liu Bang had no intention of ever returning east. But in Hanzhong, a military genius was about to change the course of history. Xiao He, Liu Bang's chancellor, recognized the extraordinary talent of a low-ranking officer named Han Xin. Under the moonlight, Xiao He chased after the departing Han Xin and persuaded him to return, then urged Liu Bang to appoint him as supreme commander. Liu Bang built a grand altar and formally invested Han Xin as Grand General in a solemn ceremony. Han Xin unveiled his master plan: outwardly repair the burned plank roads to deceive the Three Qins, while secretly leading the main army through the ancient mountain path to Chencang. In the eighth month of 206 BC, Han Xin's army burst out of the mountains and fell upon the unprepared forces of Zhang Han. The Qin veteran Zhang Han fought desperately but was pushed back to the fortress of Feiqiu. Sima Xin and Dong Yi, the other two Qin kings, surrendered without a fight. Within months, Liu Bang had reconquered the entire Guanzhong region, the old Qin heartland. With the fertile Guanzhong plain as his base and Xiao He managing logistics and recruitment, Liu Bang was now ready to challenge Xiang Yu for the empire.

Episode 3: The Pengcheng Disaster

In the spring of 205 BC, Liu Bang assembled a massive coalition army of five hundred and sixty thousand soldiers from allied kingdoms. Xiang Yu was away in the eastern state of Qi, brutally suppressing a rebellion and leaving his capital Pengcheng virtually undefended. Liu Bang's coalition swept into Pengcheng and captured the city with ease. Drunk on victory, Liu Bang seized Xiang Yu's treasures and women and held days of celebration, neglecting all military precautions. But Xiang Yu was not an ordinary general. Upon hearing that his capital had fallen, he left his main army in Qi and personally led thirty thousand elite cavalry on a lightning march back to Pengcheng. Xiang Yu's cavalry struck at dawn from the west, a direction the Han army never expected. The surprised Han forces collapsed in chaos, with tens of thousands slaughtered in the narrow streets of Pengcheng. The rout continued all the way to the Sui River, where countless fleeing soldiers drowned in the waters. Liu Bang's father and his wife Lü Zhi were captured by Chu forces and would remain prisoners for years. Liu Bang himself fled with only a handful of horsemen, and in his desperate flight he even pushed his own children out of the carriage to lighten the load. Only the loyal charioteer Xiahou Ying saved the children by repeatedly pulling them back onto the vehicle. The Pengcheng catastrophe shattered Liu Bang's coalition and sent the Han forces reeling back to the fortified city of Xingyang. It was the most humiliating defeat of Liu Bang's entire career, and the survivors who regrouped at Xingyang knew that the war was far from over.

Episode 4: The Battle of Jingxing

In the autumn of 205 BC, while Liu Bang held the line at Xingyang against Xiang Yu's relentless assaults, Han Xin marched north to open a second front. He first conquered the kingdom of Wei, capturing its king Wei Bao, and then swept through the state of Dai with astonishing speed. In the winter of 204 BC, Han Xin led thirty thousand troops into the narrow Jingxing Pass, where a Zhao army of two hundred thousand awaited him. The Zhao general Chen Yu rejected the wise counsel of his advisor Li Zuoche, who had proposed cutting Han Xin's supply lines. Confident in his overwhelming numbers, Chen Yu prepared to crush Han Xin in a single decisive battle. Han Xin did something that violated every military principle known to the ancients. He arrayed his troops with their backs to the Mianman River, leaving no line of retreat. As dawn broke, Han Xin's main force engaged the Zhao army, then feigned a retreat toward the river. The Zhao soldiers, believing victory was at hand, abandoned their fortifications to pursue the fleeing Han troops. But Han Xin had hidden two thousand cavalry behind the Zhao lines, and they seized the empty Zhao camp and replaced all the Zhao banners with the red flags of Han. When the Zhao troops turned back and saw their camp filled with Han flags, they believed they were surrounded and panicked. The trapped Zhao army was annihilated, and Chen Yu was killed on the battlefield. Han Xin treated the captured Li Zuoche with great respect and adopted his strategy for the subsequent conquest of Yan. Within a year, Han Xin had conquered Zhao, Yan, and Qi, stripping Xiang Yu of all his northern allies.

Episode 5: The Tragedy of Gaixia

By the autumn of 203 BC, four years of war had exhausted both sides and Xiang Yu agreed to divide China along the Hong Canal. But the moment Xiang Yu withdrew eastward, Liu Bang's strategists Zhang Liang and Chen Ping urged him to break the treaty and strike while the Chu army was retreating. Liu Bang attacked, but Xiang Yu turned and defeated him at Guling, and the Han forces had to wait for reinforcements. In the winter of 202 BC, Liu Bang promised Han Xin the title of King of Qi and Peng Yue the title of King of Liang. With their armies now committed, a massive Han force of six hundred thousand men converged on Xiang Yu's remaining one hundred thousand troops at Gaixia. Han Xin, commanding the great encirclement, ordered his soldiers to sing the folk songs of Chu throughout the night. Hearing their homeland melodies echoing from all directions, the homesick Chu soldiers believed that Han had already conquered all of Chu. Their morale shattered, and thousands deserted under the cover of darkness. In his tent, Xiang Yu gazed at his beloved concubine Yu Ji and sang a poem of despair: my strength could pull mountains, my spirit dominated the world, yet the times are against me and my horse will not run. Yu Ji drew a sword and took her own life rather than burden her king in his final battle. Xiang Yu led eight hundred elite horsemen in a desperate breakout charge and cut through the Han siege lines. Pursued by thousands, he was led astray by a farmer and lost his way, and by the time he reached the Wujiang River only twenty-eight riders remained. A ferryman offered to take Xiang Yu across the river to safety in his homeland of Jiangdong. But Xiang Yu refused, saying that he could not face the elders of Jiangdong after leading eight thousand of their sons to their deaths. He gave his horse to the ferryman, then turned to fight on foot, killing hundreds of Han soldiers single-handedly. Severely wounded, Xiang Yu recognized an old acquaintance among the Han ranks and declared that he would grant him the reward for his head. The Hegemon-King of Western Chu then drew his sword and cut his own throat, ending the Chu-Han Contention at the age of thirty. With Xiang Yu dead, Liu Bang unified China and founded the Han Dynasty, a civilization that would endure for four centuries and give its name to the Chinese people themselves.
