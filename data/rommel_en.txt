Episode 1: The Desert Fox Rises

In the spring of 1941, a German general arrived in Tripoli to salvage a desperate situation. His name was Erwin Rommel, and within weeks he would transform the North African campaign into a legend. The British called him the Desert Fox, a grudging tribute to his tactical brilliance and audacity. He was a master of mobile warfare, leading his Afrika Korps across the vast Libyan desert with breathtaking speed and precision.

Rommel was born in 1891 in Heidenheim, Germany. He was not a product of the Prussian military aristocracy, but the son of a schoolteacher. His rise through the ranks came from pure talent and courage. In World War I, he fought in Italy and Romania, earning the Pour le Merite, Germany\'s highest military honor, for his daring mountain assaults. He wrote a book on infantry tactics that caught Hitler\'s attention, leading to his command of a Panzer division in the 1940 invasion of France.

In North Africa, Rommel\'s genius flourished. He attacked where he was least expected, moved faster than his opponents could react, and used the desert itself as his ally. His greatest victory came at Gazala in May 1942, where he outflanked the British Eighth Army and captured Tobruk, a fortress that had held out for months. For this triumph, Hitler promoted him to Field Marshal, the youngest in the German army.

But Rommel was also a soldier of the old school, a man who fought by a code. He refused to carry out Hitler\'s Commando Order, which called for the execution of captured commandos. He treated prisoners of war according to the Geneva Conventions. And as the war turned against Germany, he became increasingly disillusioned with Hitler and the Nazi regime.

By late 1942, the tide had turned. Montgomery\'s Eighth Army defeated Rommel at El Alamein, and the Afrika Korps began its long retreat across the desert. Rommel was recalled to Germany, where he was forced to confront a terrible choice between his oath to the army and his conscience.
